To draw a three circle Venn diagram one can start with the drawing of a two circle diagram and then add a circle of equal radius and centered at one of the points of intersection of the two circles. Effectively, that is how the diagram of Figure 2.1.2 was produced. In this case the central region of intersection (the blue region of Figure 2.1.4) is known as a Reuleaux triangle. It is an example of a curve of constant width, meaning that if the object is placed between two parallel tangential lines, then the lines are equidistant, no mater the orientation of the parallel lines. This property is why they are used in the design of drill bits for drilling rounded square holes; e.g., H.J. Watts’ 1917 US patent 1,241,176. (There is a widespread misconception that the piston of Wankel engine is a Reuleaux triangle; they look similar but are not identical.)

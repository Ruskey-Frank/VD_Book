Moving on to \(n=3\text{,}\) first, what is the set of symmetries in the plane? It is not hard to see that there are 6 of them, using rotation/reflection about lines through the centers of the circles and the center of the diagram as a whole. To move things into 3 dimensions, we make the equator into a curve in Figure 4.6.8. Now what is the group of symmetries? If we join the points of intersection in Figure 4.6.10 with straight lines instead of circular arcs, then we obtain an octahedron. The dual of an octahedron is a 3-cube. Thus the symmetries are those of the cube, and so there are 48 of them! Again, there is a much richer set of symmetries when a Venn diagram is embedded on the sphere instead of the plane.

Definition 3.2.4. [rigid diagram, seperable vertex].

A vertex traversal of a vertex \(v\) in a Venn diagram is a circular sequence \(C_0, C_1, \ldots, C_m\) of the curves adjacent to \(v\text{,}\) when read in a clockwise rotation around \(v\text{.}\) A vertex is said to be separable if its vertex traversal has the property that for some \(i\text{,}\) there is a \(j\) such that the intersection of \(\mathbf{A} = C_i, C_{i+1}, \ldots, C_{j-1}\) and \(\mathbf{B} = C_j, C_{j+1}, \ldots, C_{i-1}\) consists of one curve, say \(C\text{,}\) and in addition, \(|\mathbf{A}| > 2\) and \(|\mathbf{B}| > 2\text{.}\) In the example below the sets \(\mathbf{A}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) are \(\mathbf{A}\) = { red, green, black, red, green} and \(\mathbf{B}\) = {blue, yellow, black, blue, yellow}, and the black curve is the curve \(C\text{.}\) A Venn diagram is said to be rigid if it has no separable vertices.
